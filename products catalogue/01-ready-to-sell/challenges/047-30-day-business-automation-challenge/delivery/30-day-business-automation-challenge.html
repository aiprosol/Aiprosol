The 30-Day Business Automation Challenge

Reclaim 30+ hours/week by automating one process per day for 30 days. 15 minutes a day. Real workflows. Working at the end.

Version 1.1 · 2026 · © Aiprosol Ltd · Now includes 25 importable n8n workflow JSON files

How this works

Every day for 30 days you automate one specific process. Each day is the same shape:

  • Time required: 15–25 minutes (Day 1 is 30 min for setup)
  • What you'll automate: named process, named tools
  • Why this one today: the strategic reason
  • The recipe: the exact steps
  • Success criteria: how you know it worked
  • Common pitfalls: what to watch for
  • Daily metric: what to measure

By Day 30 you have 30 working automations running in your business and you've reclaimed roughly the equivalent of one full-time employee's calendar.

Order matters. Days 1–7 build foundation tools you'll use throughout. Days 8–30 stack on top. Don't skip ahead — do them in order.

You'll need: an n8n account (self-host or cloud — free tier works for the challenge), a Google account, an email account, OpenAI API access ($5 of credit covers the whole month), optional Slack workspace. Total platform cost: $0–$30.

This pack includes 25 importable n8n workflow JSON files (in the n8n-workflows/ folder) covering many of the patterns in this challenge. Each can be imported into n8n → Workflows → Import from File, then credentials swapped for yours.

Week 1 · Foundation (Days 1–7)

Day 1 · Set up your automation control panel (30 min)

Foundation

What you'll automate: Nothing yet — you're building the cockpit.

Why this day: You can't run automations without a control panel that lets you see, edit, and debug them. If you skip this, by Day 14 you'll have automations scattered across 5 services and no way to find them.

The recipe:

  1. Sign up for n8n Cloud (or self-host — Docker one-liner: docker run -d --name n8n -p 5678:5678 docker.n8n.io/n8nio/n8n). Why n8n: open-source, fewer task limits, lets you fork and self-host when you outgrow Zapier/Make.
  2. Create a free Google Sheet called Automation Log with columns: Date · Day # · Automation · Trigger · Status · Time saved/wk · Notes.
  3. Pin the sheet to your bookmarks bar. You'll log every automation here.
  4. Create a Slack channel called #automations-log. Connect n8n to it via the built-in Slack node — you'll send every automation's first run to this channel for verification.
  5. Set up your OpenAI API key (one-time, console.openai.com/api-keys). Add $5 of credit. Save the key in n8n → Credentials.

Success criteria: You can log into n8n, you have the Sheet open, and you've successfully sent a test message to your Slack channel from an n8n test workflow.

Common pitfalls:

  • Don't get fancy with the spreadsheet — minimum viable.
  • Don't try multiple tools — pick n8n and commit. Switching mid-challenge costs days.

Daily metric: 0 (foundation day, nothing automated yet).

Day 2 · Auto-log every Stripe payment

Foundation · 15 min

What you'll automate: Stripe payments → Google Sheet row + Slack notification.

Why this day: First win. You'll instantly see what's happening in your business without logging into Stripe, and you build muscle memory with n8n's basic flow.

The recipe:

  1. In n8n: new workflow → trigger = Stripe Trigger → choose event payment_intent.succeeded.
  2. Action 1: Google Sheets · Append Row to a new sheet Sales Log with columns: Date · Customer · Amount · Product · Method · Country.
  3. Action 2: Slack · Send a Message to #automations-log with 🎉 New sale: ${{ $json.amount/100 }} from {{ $json.customer_email }}.
  4. Activate. Test by triggering a $1 test charge in Stripe (card 4242 4242 4242 4242 in test mode).

Success criteria: Test payment lands → row appears in sheet within 2 seconds → Slack message arrives.

Common pitfalls: Stripe and n8n use different webhook signatures. n8n's Stripe Trigger handles this; if you went HTTP-Request route instead, you'd need to verify the Stripe-Signature header manually.

Daily metric: Time to manual sales review: typically 15 min/day → now 0. Saving: 75 min/wk.

Day 3 · Auto-tag every inbound email

Foundation · 20 min

What you'll automate: Gmail → AI categorisation → Auto-label.

The recipe:

  1. n8n workflow → trigger = Gmail Trigger (poll every 5 min).
  2. Filter: from does NOT contain your own domain (skip your own emails).
  3. Action: OpenAI · Chat with prompt: "Classify this email into exactly one category: SALES_LEAD, CUSTOMER_SUPPORT, BILLING, VENDOR, PERSONAL, NEWSLETTER, OTHER. Return only the category name. Subject: {{ $json.subject }}. Body: {{ $json.snippet }}".
  4. Switch node by AI output: each branch applies the corresponding Gmail label.
  5. Optional: high-priority categories (SALES_LEAD, CUSTOMER_SUPPORT) trigger a Slack ping.

Success criteria: Send yourself test emails of each type. They land with the right label within 5 minutes.

Common pitfalls: Gmail labels must exist before n8n can apply them. Create all 7 labels manually first. OpenAI step costs ~$0.001 per email × 100/day × 30 days = $3. Worth it.

Daily metric: Inbox triage time: 30 min/day → 5 min. Saving: 175 min/wk.

Day 4 · Auto-respond to FAQ-shape emails

Foundation · 25 min

What you'll automate: Common-question emails → AI-drafted reply queued to your inbox for one-click send.

The recipe:

  1. Build on Day 3's classifier. When category = CUSTOMER_SUPPORT or SALES_LEAD, branch.
  2. Add OpenAI · Chat node: feed the email body + your top 10 FAQs (paste into the system prompt) → ask for a draft reply.
  3. Output: Gmail · Create Draft (NOT send — draft to your inbox).
  4. Slack ping: "Draft reply queued for [subject] — review at gmail.com/drafts".

Success criteria: FAQ-shape email triggers a reasonable draft reply in your Drafts folder within 5 min.

Pitfalls: Set the AI prompt to ALWAYS produce a draft (not full auto-send) until you've reviewed 20+ drafts and trust the quality. Then you can flip to auto-send for high-confidence categories.

Daily metric: Email drafting time: 90 min/day → 15 min review. Saving: 525 min/wk.

Day 7 · Daily metrics digest

Foundation · 25 min

What you'll automate: Every morning, a single Slack message with yesterday's numbers.

The recipe:

  1. n8n workflow, trigger: Schedule Trigger — every day at 8:00.
  2. Multiple parallel branches:
    • Pull yesterday's Stripe revenue (Stripe · Search)
    • Pull yesterday's lead count (count rows in your Leads sheet/CRM)

  3. Aggregate into one Slack message: 📊 Yesterday: $X revenue · Y leads · Z customer emails. [view dashboard].

Daily metric: Time spent compiling daily numbers: 22 min → 0. Saving: 110 min/wk.

Week 2 · Sales & customer (Days 8–14)

Day 8 · Lead-source attribution

Sales · 20 min

What you'll automate: Auto-tag every lead with where they came from (UTM params, referer, manual entry).

Why today: If you don't know which marketing channel produced a lead, you can't double-down on what works. This builds the data layer that powers every future marketing decision.

The recipe:

  1. On your lead-capture form, append hidden fields: utm_source, utm_medium, utm_campaign, referrer (all read via JavaScript from window.location.search and document.referrer).
  2. Extend the Day 5 workflow: read those fields → store as custom properties on the HubSpot contact.
  3. Build a second weekly workflow: Schedule Trigger (Mondays) → query HubSpot by utm_source → aggregate counts and conversion rates → post a Slack digest.
  4. For leads with no UTMs, mark source=organic-direct. Don't lose them.

Success criteria: Submit a test lead from a URL with ?utm_source=test. The lead appears in HubSpot with utm_source=test attached.

Pitfalls: UTMs only survive same-tab navigation by default. Use first-party cookies (utm_source stored on first page-view, persisted across the visit) if you want multi-page attribution.

Daily metric: Time to answer "where did this lead come from?": 5–15 min of digging → 0.

Day 9 · No-show prevention

Sales · 15 min

What you'll automate: Day-before reminders for booked calls; if no confirmation, flag for personal outreach. Reduces no-shows 60–80%.

The recipe:

  1. n8n trigger: Schedule Trigger — every day at 17:00.
  2. Action: Google Calendar · Get Events for tomorrow's date range.
  3. Loop: for each meeting with an external attendee, send a personalised reminder email — include the agenda, your LinkedIn, and the reschedule link.
  4. Sub-workflow: 2 hours before the meeting, check if the attendee has opened the reminder (via Gmail · Search for the message ID + read receipt). If not, send a Slack ping to the rep: "Heads up — {Name} hasn't opened tomorrow's reminder. Worth a personal text."

Success criteria: Schedule a test meeting tomorrow. By 17:00 you receive the reminder; by morning the rep gets the Slack heads-up if you didn't open it.

Daily metric: Average no-show rate. Industry baseline 24%; well-implemented this gets to 5–8%.

Day 13 · Renewal reminder

Customer · 20 min

What you'll automate: 60 days before any subscription renewal, customer gets a personalised "here's what you got from us" email + retention offer.

The recipe:

  1. n8n trigger: Schedule Trigger — daily at 9am.
  2. Action: Stripe · Search Subscriptions where current_period_end is between today+59 and today+61 days.
  3. For each: pull customer's 12-month usage summary (from your product analytics).

  5. Output: Gmail · Create Draft (NOT auto-send for the first 5 — review the AI quality before flipping to auto).

Success criteria: A test subscription expiring in 60 days surfaces a draft email today.

Daily metric: Renewal rate. Companies that pre-empt renewals with usage summaries see 8–18% improvement in retention.

Day 27 · Hire-pipeline triage

Operations · 25 min

What you'll automate: Inbound applications → AI-screened against role criteria → top 5 surfaced, rest auto-rejected with a kind template.

The recipe:

  1. n8n trigger: Webhook for your applicant tracking system (or a Gmail trigger for careers@).
  2. Extract the resume text (use OpenAI vision on PDF if needed).
  3. AI score against the JD: return { score, top_strengths[3], concerns[2], recommendation }.
  4. Switch by score: ≥80 → Slack ping hiring manager with brief; 50–79 → queue for human review; <50 → send the rejection-with-encouragement email template.

Pitfalls: Resume screening is one of the most legally-fraught automations. Consult your HR / legal counsel before turning it on. ALWAYS keep a human in the final loop. NEVER auto-reject without human review for the first 100 applications — calibrate first.

Daily metric: Time to first-pass screen: 4–10 min/applicant → 0.

Day 30 · The meta-automation

Compounding · 25 min

What you'll automate: The automation that monitors your other automations.

Why this day: By Day 29 you have ~29 workflows running. If you don't have a meta-layer watching them, the first time one silently breaks you won't notice for weeks. Today fixes that forever.

The recipe:

  1. n8n trigger: Schedule Trigger — every Monday at 7am.
  2. Query the n8n API (GET /executions) for last week's runs across all workflows.
  3. Aggregate by workflow: total runs, success rate, average duration, $ estimated cost.
  4. Flag any workflow with: failure rate > 5%, no runs at all in 7 days (probably broken silently), cost spike > 2× previous week.
  5. AI compile a one-page "automation health report" markdown.
  6. Output: Notion · Create Page + Slack ping.

Success criteria: Next Monday a single page lists every automation, its status, and any flags. You can spot a broken workflow in 30 seconds.

Daily metric: Time from a workflow breaking to noticing: previously weeks → now < 7 days max.

Bonus: Extend this meta-automation to track time saved per workflow (use your Day 1 Sheet). Compounding ROI made visible.

What to do on Day 31 and beyond

  1. Stop building for 2 weeks. Let what you built run. Watch the Day 30 health report. Tune anything flagged.
  2. Then build one more per week, not one per day. The compounding gains come from quality, not quantity.
  3. Document everything. Each automation gets a short doc: trigger, what it does, who to ping if it breaks, how to disable.
  4. Plan a 90-day audit. Some of your Day 1–7 automations will be outdated. Refresh them.
